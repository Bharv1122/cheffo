Learn / Safety

Is homemade dog food safe?

Short answer: yes — when it's balanced and avoids the toxic-food list. Dogs have eaten home-prepared food for far longer than kibble has existed. The risk isn't "homemade"; the risk is unbalanced homemade.

The two things that actually go wrong

1. Nutritional gaps. A widely cited UC Davis study analyzed 200 homemade dog food recipes from books and websites and found the overwhelming majority were deficient in at least one essential nutrient. The most common gap is calcium — meat is naturally high in phosphorus and low in calcium, so a "chicken and rice" bowl that looks wholesome can quietly starve a dog's skeleton over months. Zinc, vitamin D, and omega-3 fatty acids are frequent runners-up.

2. Toxic ingredients. Some perfectly normal human foods are dangerous to dogs. The ones to treat as hard bans:

Never feed: chocolate, grapes & raisins, onion, garlic (including onion/garlic powder — check broth labels), xylitol (in many peanut butters and "sugar-free" products), macadamia nuts, alcohol, caffeine, avocado, raw yeast dough, nutmeg.

Xylitol and grapes deserve special fear: small amounts can be life-threatening, and xylitol hides in products people genuinely believe are dog-friendly, like peanut butter.

Can a dog eat only homemade food?

Yes — plenty of dogs thrive on fully home-prepared diets. The requirement is that the diet is complete and balanced over time, which essentially never happens by winging it. That means recipes formulated against an established nutrient reference (AAFCO profiles are the standard one in the US), an appropriate calcium source, and sensible variety rather than the same single recipe forever.

Do I need supplements?

Usually some, used surgically. The philosophy we'd argue for: real food first, supplements only where food can't get there. Calcium is the classic example — it's genuinely hard to hit the target with food alone unless you're comfortable with ground eggshell or bone meal, measured properly. A recipe that "needs" fifteen supplements is a spreadsheet wearing a dinner's clothes; a recipe that pretends it needs none is probably deficient.

The non-negotiable step: your vet

Before any major diet change, run it past a veterinarian who knows your dog — especially for puppies (growth makes calcium errors much more damaging), seniors, pregnant or nursing dogs, and any dog with a medical condition or prescription diet. This isn't a liability disclaimer; a vet can see things about your specific dog that no recipe, app, or article can.

A sane safety checklist

  1. Formulate against a nutrient standard — don't improvise ratios.
  2. Include a real calcium source; confirm the calcium-to-phosphorus ratio.
  3. Screen every ingredient against the toxic list above.
  4. Cook meat to safe internal temperatures (lightly cooked, not raw).
  5. Transition gradually over about a week to avoid stomach upset.
  6. Have your vet review the plan, and recheck weight and body condition.
