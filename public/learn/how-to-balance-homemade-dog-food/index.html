How to balance homemade dog food

"Balanced" means hitting nutrient targets over time — not vibes. The single most common failure in homemade dog food is calcium, and it's invisible: the bowl looks perfect while the ratio is upside down.

Why "looks healthy" isn't enough

A UC Davis study analyzed 200 homemade dog food recipes from books and websites — including some written by vets — and found nearly all fell short on at least one essential nutrient. That's not because cooking for dogs is a bad idea. It's because "chicken, rice, and some carrots" describes an ingredient list, not a formulation. Balance is arithmetic, and almost nobody does the arithmetic by hand.

The calcium problem, in one paragraph

Meat is rich in phosphorus and nearly empty of calcium. Dogs need calcium and phosphorus together, in roughly a 1.2 : 1 ratio for adults. So a meat-heavy homemade diet without an added calcium source doesn't just miss the calcium target — it actively inverts the ratio, and the dog's body responds by pulling calcium from bone. Nothing looks wrong for months. The fix is boring and cheap: a measured calcium source (ground eggshell, bone meal, or a canine calcium supplement) dosed to the recipe. Puppies are far less forgiving of calcium errors than adult dogs — growing skeletons make this the one place to be strict, and a place your vet should sign off.

What a balanced recipe actually covers

  • Protein — quality animal protein covering all essential amino acids; it anchors the recipe.
  • Fat — energy plus essential fatty acids; omega-3s (EPA/DHA from oily fish or fish oil) are the usual gap.
  • Calcium & phosphorus — right amounts and right ratio, as above.
  • Micronutrients — vitamin D, vitamin E, zinc, iodine, and B vitamins are the ones that most often need attention depending on ingredients.
  • Carbohydrates & fiber — no strict requirement, but sweet potato, rice, or oats are practical energy and fiber sources once protein and fat targets are met.

The reference standard worth knowing by name: AAFCO nutrient profiles (Association of American Feed Control Officials), which define minimum nutrient levels by life stage. "Formulated against AAFCO profiles" is the difference between a recipe and a guess.

Food first, supplements second

Two failure modes to avoid. One is the recipe that pretends whole foods cover everything — it almost certainly doesn't, starting with calcium. The other is the recipe that's really a supplement stack with some chicken in it. The defensible middle: let real food do the heavy lifting, then add only the specific supplements that close the remaining gaps, in measured doses. If you can't say why a supplement is in the bowl, it shouldn't be.

How to do this without a spreadsheet

Practically, you have three options: hire a board-certified veterinary nutritionist (the gold standard, especially for dogs with medical conditions), use a formulation tool that does the nutrient math against AAFCO targets, or accept that hand-built recipes need professional review. What doesn't work is trusting a random blog recipe — that's the exact category the UC Davis study tested.
