Lightly cooked vs raw vs kibble

The honest version: kibble is complete but ultra-processed, raw is fresh but carries real pathogen risk, and lightly cooked is the middle path — whole ingredients, cooked to safe temperatures. Each has a real trade-off; anyone who says otherwise is selling something.

What kibble gets right (yes, really)

Complete-and-balanced kibble meets defined nutrient standards (AAFCO profiles in the US), stores for months, and requires zero thought. Dogs live long lives on it. If someone tells you kibble is poison, stop listening — that's marketing, not nutrition. The honest criticisms are narrower: it's ultra-processed, cooked at very high heat, optimized for shelf life over freshness, and many dogs are simply bored by it. Those are real reasons to want better; they're not a reason to panic.

The problem with raw

Raw feeding gets the "whole fresh food" part right and the microbiology wrong. Raw meat can carry Salmonella, Listeria, and E. coli — risks the FDA, CDC, and major veterinary organizations including the AVMA have all warned about. And it's not only the dog at risk: contamination spreads to bowls, counters, and the humans in the kitchen, which matters extra in homes with kids, elderly family members, or anyone immunocompromised. The frustrating part is that the benefits raw feeders want don't require rawness.

Why lightly cooked is the sane middle

Gentle cooking to safe internal temperatures kills the pathogens while keeping most of what makes fresh food worth feeding: recognizable whole ingredients, high moisture, strong palatability. You lose a little heat-sensitive nutrition versus raw (modest, and a properly formulated recipe accounts for it) and you gain a food you can prepare in a normal kitchen with normal hygiene. Raw fruits and vegetables — carrots, blueberries, cucumber — stay raw; they were never the problem.

The catch, and it's the same catch as raw: homemade anything must be balanced deliberately. A lightly cooked diet that ignores calcium or nutrient targets is worse than decent kibble. Balance is the price of admission — see how to balance homemade dog food.

A low-risk way to start

You don't have to convert overnight. Start with fresh food as a topper or 25–50% of calories (reduce the kibble to keep total calories steady), watch your dog's weight and enthusiasm for a few weeks, then decide whether to go further. Transition gradually over about a week either way.
